Cover Story12 min

Riso thinking for product teams

Independent publishers have known for years that constraints can become the voice. A two-color palette, a misregistered image, and a tight deadline can make a page feel alive. Digital teams are borrowing that energy for launches, reports, and editorial microsites.

The best examples do not imitate paper as decoration. They use paper logic to guide pacing: one dominant headline, a marked correction, a taped visual, and a quiet column that lets the reader breathe.

Interview8 min

The art director keeps the crooked scan

Mara Voss runs a three-person magazine from a studio above a bicycle repair shop. Her rule is simple: if a texture helps the reader understand where a piece came from, it stays.

"Clean is not the same as clear," she says. The studio now prototypes page rhythm in browser first, then prints only the spreads that deserve ink.

"The mess only works when the editor still has a spine."

ProcessStudio Visit

Margins as navigation

Instead of hiding wayfinding in a smooth top bar, the zine uses visible issue stamps, table-of-contents blocks, and repeated section labels. Readers always know whether they are at the cover, the photo desk, or the letters page.

Criticism6 min

Against fake authenticity

A torn edge is not a personality. The physical language has to support editorial judgment: hierarchy first, material cues second, motion last. If a page gets harder to read because it wants to look handmade, the gimmick wins and the reader loses.

This skeleton keeps article bodies on generous paper surfaces and pulls the expressive behavior into edges, rotations, stamps, tape, and transitions.

Layout rules

Chaos with an editor.

  • 01

    Every page needs one loud idea. Rotations, tape, and overlaps are accents around that idea, not a substitute for it.

  • 02

    Article surfaces stay calm enough to read. Texture belongs in the paper, the edges, and the image treatment.

  • 03

    Mobile collapses into stacked cutouts with reduced overlap, larger reading areas, and full-width controls.
